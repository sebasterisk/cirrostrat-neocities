▸ the spanish rail network, and a lesson on navigation

I've never told this narrative to many... so let me tell you a tale about trains, timetables, and navigating a network not really meant for us. Oh, and churros. Back in the spring of '23 I was priviledged enough to travel to the country with arguably the second-most best high speed rail network: Spain. And, oh boy, that was one heck of a whirlwind.

Immediately after school the day spring break had begun, we had went directly to IAD for our flight to BCN via BRU. While that leg of the trip was unspectacular at best, I had landed in Barcelona 26 hours without sleep and still had another 4 hours until I could reach the sweet relief of a matress.

Here came our first test of interpreting maps. We had needed to go from the airport station to Sant Vicenç de Calders, which is only served by the R2 Sud and R4 (though numerous regional express lines cover it). For some reason, the R2 is split into three variants (but for some reason there are more which I'll get to in a second). We had to coordinate a weird double-back manuver as the airport is only served by R2 Nord as a stub station.

We transferred to the reverse direction at the station immediately following Aeroport on the main line. And we were on our way to the destination... unless...

LESSON 1 | PAY ATTENTION TO THE DESTINATION

I increasingly became much more worried once I realized that this train ain't goin' all the way to the end. Yeah... not all R2 Sud trains are created equally, and I failed to see this immediately. YOU CAN'T BLAME ME THOUGH I WAS SLEEP DEPRIVED and EVERYTHING was in CATALAN! We ended up stopping short of Sant Vicenç de Calders and was stuck for a good half-hour at Vilanova i la Geltrú.

Remember when I said the R2 had three variants? Well, actually, there are 12. None of which are clearly communicated on maps except for the 3 main ones. And the R2 Sud trains that go to Sant Vicenç de Calders does NOT stop at the station we caught the line at, El Prat de Llobregat.

I think the commuter rail system map needs to reflect this more, because unless one actually goes and reads timetables (which, be honest, who does?), but i digress...

Yet, if I actually took the time to read the headboard of the train, though, I should have known that this train would not have taken me all the way. Destinations of trains... anywhere, really... are often clearly marked on displays so it would have been hard to miss if I were not so ignorant. Fortunately it wasn't a "last train of the day"-type situation so we were able to wait for the next one, and all was well.
